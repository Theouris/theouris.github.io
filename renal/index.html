Total body water is 60% of body weight. Of this, 2/3 is intracellular fluid and 1/3 is extracellular. Of extracellular fluid, 25% is plasma and 75% is interstitial fluid. The boundary between ECF and ICF is the cell membrane, and the only things that move readily across are water and nonpolar substances. Electrolytes require carrier/channel proteins and are affected by Starling forces and increases in membrane permeability (inflammation).

Intravascular volume, or blood volume, is what determines BP and cardiac output. Blood volume is plasma volume plus RBC volume, which is about 5-6L on average. Hematocrit is the percent of blood volume that consists of red blood cells (typically 35-45%).

Fluid Imbalance

A marked increase in body fluid volume is called volume expansion. Conversely, a decrease is called volume contraction. There are many different causes of both volume expansion and contraction; most of the osmoles in ECF are due to sodium, and changes in sodium concentrations will have significant impact on volume.

Depending on underlying pathology, the IV fluid used for resuscitation will vary. Isotonic fluids have the same osmolar concentration as plasma (normal Saline, Ringer's Lactate, Whole Blood, Plasma, 5% Albumin). Hypertonic fluids have an osmolar concentration higher than plasma (3 percent NaCl). Hypotonic fluids have an osmolar concentration lower than plasma (1/2 Normal Saline, Water). Usually, the most important thing to do to treat a patient is to restore intravascular volume; this is accomplished with normal saline or lactate ringer.

Blood loss is classified into four stages based on the percentage of blood volume lost and the resulting physiologic response. In Class I (0-15% blood loss, about 750 mL), patients typically maintain normal vital signs, with a normal or minimally elevated heart rate (around 100 bpm), normal blood pressure, and only mild anxiety. In Class II (15-30% loss, about 750-1500 mL), compensatory mechanisms become more apparent, with tachycardia (100-120 bpm) and an elevated respiratory rate (20-24 breaths per minute); pulse pressure begins to narrow, although systolic blood pressure may still remain normal. In Class III (30-40% loss, about 1500-2000 mL), decompensation develops, characterized by a significant drop in systolic blood pressure, heart rate exceeding 120 bpm, rapid respirations, confusion, and decreased urine output due to reduced organ perfusion. Finally, Class IV (40% minimum blood lost, which is around 2000 mL) represents severe, life-threatening shock with marked tachycardia (140+ bpm), profound hypotension, altered mental status progressing to lethargy, and cold, pale skin secondary to intense peripheral vasoconstriction.

Sodium Imbalance

Sodium disorders primarily cause nonspecific neurologic symptoms due to shifts in cellular water balance that lead to brain cell swelling (hyponatremia) or shrinkage (hypernatremia). Clinical manifestations may include impaired consciousness, generalized weakness, anorexia, nausea and vomiting, headache, and varying degrees of mental status changes, ranging from confusion to coma depending on severity. Diabetes Insipidus, which is the lack of or response to ADH (vasopressin), is one such way hypernatremia can occur.

Hyponatremia can be caused by euvolemic (same volume), hypovolemic, and hypervolemic problems. SIADH (or the syndrome of inappropriate ADH excretion), hypothyroidism, and polydipsia are all euvolemic causes of hyponatremia. SIADH is a paraneoplastic syndrome that leads to increased water excretion and increased urine sodium. Hypothyroidism decreases cardiac output and, by extension, renal perfusion. Even with normal fluid volume, decreased perfusion increases water retention, causing hyponatremia.

Hypovolemic hyponatremia can only occur in those who lose more salt than water. Examples of this are diarrhea, thiazide diuretic use, and adrenal insufficiency. Adrenal insufficiency causes a marked decrease in aldosterone and cortisol, both of which contribute to sodium retention.

Hypervolemic hyponatremia occurs in patients with increased total body sodium and water, but with decreased effective arterial blood volume (intravascular volume), leading to ADH-mediated water retention and diluted serum sodium. Common causes include nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis, and sepsis.

Potassium Problems

The majority (2/3) of potassium is reabsorbed in the PCT of the nephron. Another 20-25% is reabsorbed in the ascending loop of Henle, and (depending on diet) the rest of potassium is reabsorbed in the DCT. Excretion of potassium is dependent on diet, aldosterone, acid-base balance, flow rate, and plasma osmolarity.

Potassium typically follows the flow of water and is further affected by the aforementioned factors. Insulin and beta-2 agonists cause the flow of potassium into the ICF via activation of Na+/K+ ATPase. Hyperosmolarity of ECF causes water (which is followed by potassium) to flow out of cells. Exercise and cell lysis also cause potassium to enter the ECF. Moreover, acid-base balances can cause potassium to flow in/out of the cell (due to H+/K+ exchangers).

One key concept to know is that, unlike with sodium, you do not need to hesitate to restore potassium levels in hypokalemic patients. Hypokalemia can be treated with either oral or IV potassium.

Calcium

About 40% of calcium is bound to proteins (primarily albumin) in the blood plasma, while 50% of total body calcium is free calcium. Free calcium (Ca2+) is the active, ionized form of calcium that is involved with metabolic actions in the body.

Hypercalcemia is primarily caused by too much parathyroid hormone (PTH), which can be due to paraneoplastic syndrome or due to hyperplasia of the parathyroid glands. Bone metastasis, vitamin D excess, sarcoidosis, thiazide diuretics, and milk alkali syndrome (too much ingested calcium) can also cause hypercalcemia. Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcemia (FHH) is a benign condition in which the calcium sensing mechanism is dysfunctional, meaning the baseline calcium levels of the body are set higher and PTH is slightly, chronically elevated.

Hypercalcemia can cause nausea, vomiting, constipation, fatigue, weakness, polydipsia, polyuria, and a shortened QT interval. It can be treated with fluids, diuretics, calcitonin, biphosphate, and (in extreme cases) parathyroidectomy.

Hypocalcemia is caused by either too little PTH (which is due to either trauma or surgery), renal failure, dietary problems, or acute pancreatitis. Hypocalcemia causes paresthesia (pins and needles feeling), Chvostek's sign (facial twitching in response to tapping the facial nerve), Trousseau's sign (wrist spasm in response to inflating a blood pressure cuff), tetany, prolonged QT, and death. Hypocalcemia can be treated with oral supplements and vitamin D.

Some important concepts to understand regarding calcium serum levels are: loop diuretics will decrease serum calcium, thiazide diuretics will increase serum calcium, and alkalosis will increase the amount of serum proteins that free calcium can bind to and cause hypocalcemia.

Phosphate Problems

Hyperphosphatemia is caused by renal failure to excrete phosphate. Can also be caused by hypoparathyroidism. Hypophosphatemia can be caused by too much phosphate lost in the urine (which is usually due to primary hyperparathyroidism and Fanconi syndrome), not enough phosphate absorbed in the GI tract, and refeeding syndrome.

Refeeding syndrome occurs when someone has not eaten for an extended period of time. Starvation leads to the depletion of important electrolytes (Potassium, Magnesium, Phosphate) leading to low levels in both the ICF and ECF. When insulin rises rapidly once individuals in starvation mode are fed, the cellular response to insulin causes electrolytes to quickly move into the cell, making the electrolytes in the ECF critically low.

Parathyroid Hormone and the Ca++/Phosphate Axis

Chief cells in the parathyroid gland secrete PTH when stimulated by low Ca++. PTH has three main sites of action: bones, intestines, and kidneys. PTH at the site of bones stimulates osteoclast activity to release Ca++ from bone ECM. PTH at the kidneys facilitates phosphate excretion, Ca++ resorption, and is specifically where Vitamin D is converted to its active form. Vitamin D at the intestines increases Ca++ and phosphate absorption, promotes Ca++ and phosphate mobilization from the bone, and further promotes Ca++ and phosphate absorption in the kidneys. A lack of vitamin D leads to Ricketts.

Primary Hyperparathyroidism is the autonomous excretion of PTH, typically due to parathyroid adenoma, and causes "bones, stones, groans, and moans" due to the increase in Ca++ and decreased phosphate. Bones refers to bone pain, osteoporosis, and increased likelihood of fracture. Stones refers to kidney stones (calcium oxalate and calcium phosphate). Groans refers to the abdominal pain caused by constipation and nausea. Finally, moans refers to neurological symptoms caused by hypercalcemia.

Secondary hyperparathyroidism is caused by a response to decreased Ca++. This is most importantly due to renal failure. Secondary hyperparathyroidism is characterized by low Ca++ and high phosphate. Tertiary hyperparathyroidism is caused by renal failure/secondary hyperparathyroidism, and is the autonomous excretion of PTH just like as seen in primary hyperparathyroidism.

Hypoparathyroidism is caused by damage to the parathyroid glands during a thyroidectomy. It can also be due to a congenital disease called DiGeorge syndrome in which the 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouches fail to develop. A similar association to hypoparathyroidism, pseudohypoparathyroidism, is due to a defect in the PTH receptor in the PCT. PTH levels are high, but phosphate levels in the urine are low.

Acid-Base Balancing

The human body needs to maintain a constant pH in order to properly function. Some important acid-base balancing equations to know for Step are: the Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation, Plasma anion gap, Serum osmolality, and Winter's Formula.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation: pH=6.1+log([HCO3-] / 0.03×PCO2)

Anion gap = [Na+]-([Cl-]+[HCO3-])

Serum osmolality = 2[Na+] + 18glucose + 2.8BUN

Winter's: Expected PaCO2 = (1.5 × HCO3-) + 8 ± 2

Overview

The nephron is the structural unit of the kidney. In flow order, the nephron consists of a glomerulus, bowman capsule, proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), Loop of Henle, distal convoluted tubule (DCT), and the collecting duct. The collecting duct collects urine from numerous nephrons, and is not part of the proper nephron. The body of the nephron (capsule, PCT, DCT) and the medullary rays (collecting ducts + straight tubule segments) are contained in the cortex of the kidney. The medulla of the kidney lies beneath the cortex, and contains the Loop of Henle, Collecting ducts, and renal pyramids (organized medullary units). Cortical necrosis refers to necrosis in the glomerulus/Bowman's capsule of the renal cortex, and papillary necrosis refers to necrosis in the medulla.

The renal pyramid is within the medulla, but the renal columns, which lie in parallel, are still part of the cortex. The renal lobe consists of the pyramid and the cortex above it. The renal papillae are the tips of the renal pyramids, pointing inwards towards the minor calyx, where urine collection occurs. Minor calyxes synapse into major calyxes, then the renal pelvis and ultimately the ureter. The renal sinus contains the renal pelvis, calyxes, vessels, nerves, and fat.

The blood that flows to the kidney starts in the renal artery. Then to the segmental artery, interlobar artery, arcuate artery, interlobular artery, afferent arteriole, glomerulus, efferent arteriole, then either the vasa recta or peritubular capillaries, then to the interlobular vein, the arcuate vein, interlobar vein, and finally leaves via the renal vein. A renal lobe is a renal pyramid with its cortex above, and a renal lobule is the collecting duct and all its nephrons (a microscopic unit). There is a difference between interlobar arteries and interlobular arteries. The peritubular capillaries (PCT and DCT) and the vasa recta (Loop of Henle) are both sites of reabsorption in the nephron.

Histology

Filtration occurs at the glomerulus of the kidney. Reabsorption and secretion occurs at the tubules. Urine concentration occurs at the Loop of Henle and collecting duct. Simple squamous cells line the epithelium of the glomerulus, and are designed for facilitating filtration. Simple cuboidal cells of the PCT and DCT are great at reabsorption due to an abundance of protein channels present in the membrane. Transitional epithelium is great with handling a stretch response.

The renal corpuscle consists of the glomerulus (capillary network) and the Bowman capsule (first site of filtrate collection). The vascular pole of the corpuscle consists of the afferent and efferent arterioles, and the urinary pole consists of the Bowman's capsule of filtrate continuous with the PCT. The glomerulus consists of fenestrated capillaries between afferent and efferent arterioles, and also contains mesangial cells that provide structural support, phagocytosis, and have contractile capabilities for regulating Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).

The Bowman's capsule consists of a visceral layer in contact with the glomerulus. This layer has a specific cell type called podocytes that form filtration slits to regulate what compounds can/cannot be filtered. The parietal layer is the outer layer of the capsule, and is composed of simple squamous epithelium that is continuous with the simple cuboidal epithelium of the PCT. The Bowman's space lies between the visceral and parietal layers, and contains the filtrate that is then passed on to the PCT.

The filtration barrier has three layers: a fenestrated capillary endothelium, a glomerular basement membrane (GBM), and the podocytes. Understanding these three layers is paramount for understanding the underlying pathology for numerous renal diseases; nephritic syndrome is due to glomerular inflammation, nephrotic syndrome is due to podocyte damage, proteinuria is also due to podocyte damage, Alport syndrome is due to a GBM defect, and IgA nephropathy causes mesangial proliferation.

The proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) consists of a simple cuboidal epithelium with a prominent apical brush border (due to abundant microvilli). Abundant mitochondria are also present in these cells, leading to eosinophilic staining of their cytoplasm. The function of the PCT is to reabsorb the secreted Na+ (two-thirds of all secreted Sodium), water, glucose, amino acids, and bicarbonate. H+, drugs, and toxins are all secreted in the PCT as well. Fanconi syndrome is a disease in which the PCT fails to properly reabsorb the metabolites mentioned above.

Water leaves the filtrate in the descending Loop of Henle (which makes the filtrate hyperosmotic). In histology, a simple cuboidal epithelium also lines the thick descending limb. The thin descending limb of Henle is lined with a simple squamous epithelium and is much more permeable to water; this is where the filtrate becomes highly concentrated. In the ascending Loop of Henle, salt leaves the filtrate, making it more hypoosmotic. The thin ascending limb is also a simple squamous epithelium, except now it is impermeable to water and facilitates passive NaCl reabsorption. The thick ascending limb is a simple cuboidal epithelium with no brush border, water impermeable, and active Na+-K+-2Cl- cotransporters. The loop of Henle runs parallel to the vasa recta, which is a countercurrent network of capillaries that help make the filtrate hyperosmotic in the descending loop and hypoosmotic in the ascending loop.

The distal convoluted tubule (DCT) consists of simple cuboidal epithelium with no brush border, a larger lumen than the PCT, and more visible nuclei. It is largely impermeable to water and facilitates NaCl reabsorption. The late DCT is aldosterone-sensitive and increases Na+ resorption under stimulation. Macula densa cells are also present in the DCT and sense tubular NaCl; low levels of NaCl are sensed by the macula densa and then prompt renin release from the juxtaglomerular (JG) cells of the afferent arteriole.

The Juxtaglomerular Apparatus (JGA) consists of the Macula densa, the JG cells, and the extraglomerular mesangial cells. Under low NaCl stimulation, the Macula densa stimulates the JG cells to secrete renin, triggering RAAS. Under high stimulation, the JG cells release adenosine and ATP, causing afferent arteriole vasoconstriction and decreased GFR.

The collecting ducts transition from simple cuboidal cells to simple columnar. They display distinct cell borders and pale cytoplasm with centrally-located nuclei. Principal cells consist of 70% of the collecting duct. These cells facilitate water resorption under ADH stimulation and Na+ resorption with K+ secretion under Aldosterone stimulation. 30% of the collecting duct consists of intercalated cells; alpha intercalated cells facilitate H+ secretion, and beta intercalated cells facilitate bicarbonate secretion.

The renal papilla is the tip of the renal pyramid, where collecting ducts empty into the minor calyxes. The renal pelvis is the dilated portion of the ureter located within the kidney, and is lined by transitional epithelium (aka urothelium) that allows stretching.

The ureter is lined by urothelium, which consists of Umbrella cells (multinucleated epithelial cells that form the outermost layer of the urothelium of the bladder, ureters, and renal pelvis) intermediate cells, and a basal cell layer. The lumen of the ureter forms a star shape. The muscles that surround the ureter (inner longitudinal, outer circular, then an outermost, distal longitudinal muscle) are used to facilitate unidirectional urine flow. The detrusor muscle that surrounds the bladder consists of these layers as well.

The urethral epithelium varies along its length and differs between males and females. In females, the proximal urethra near the bladder is lined by transitional epithelium (urothelium), while more distal portions are primarily stratified squamous epithelium, with some areas containing stratified columnar epithelium. In males, the prostatic urethra is lined by transitional epithelium, which transitions to pseudostratified columnar epithelium in the membranous urethra. The spongy (penile) urethra is mainly lined by pseudostratified columnar epithelium, with the terminal portion transitioning to stratified squamous epithelium, which becomes keratinized at the external urethral meatus. Since the female urethra is shorter, women tend to have an increased risk for UTIs.

Male Reproductive Anatomy

The male reproductive system consists of the testes, epididymis, vas deferens, ejaculatory ducts, urethra, and penis. Accessory glands to the male reproductive system include the prostate gland, the seminal vesicles, and the bulbourethral (cowper's) glands.

The testes consists of the tunica albuginea (the dense fibrous capsule that surrounds the testis and sends septa inward to divide the testis into lobules), The tunica vaginalis (serous sac that surrounds the testes), and the actual testicle itself. The testes are innervated by the testicular plexus (autonomic nerves), sympathetic fibers from T10-T11, and afferent pain fibers that travel to T10-T11 and refer pain to those dermatome regions.

The testicles are supplied by the testicular artery, which branches off the abdominal aorta at L2. This artery travels within the spermatic cord. The pampiniform plexus forms the testicular vein; the right testicular vein drains into the IVC, and the left testicular vein drains into the left renal vein. The lymph of the testes drains directly into the lumbar (para-aortic) lymph nodes.

The epididymis connects the testes to the ductus deferens. It stores sperm until ejaculation. It consists of a head (superior), body, and tail that connects to the vas deferens. It has the same innervation as the testes. The ductus deferens (vas deferens) ascends with the spermatic cord through the inguinal canal, and passes lateral to the inferior epigastric artery (and anterior to the external iliac vessels as well as the ureters) to join the seminal vesicle in forming the ejaculatory ducts. The ejaculatory ducts insert into the urethra just superior to the prostate and are responsible for putting semen in the urethra.

The prostate is the structure in male anatomy that surrounds the prostatic urethra and is inferior to the bladder. The urethra penetrates the transitional zone of the prostate, which is the location where benign prostate hyperplasia occurs. The central zones surround the ejaculatory ducts and the peripheral zone is lateral (is also the site of most prostate cancers). Prostatic arteries arise from branches of the internal iliac arteries (inferior vesicle, middle rectal, and internal pudendal arteries). A prostatic venous plexus drains the prostate, and is continuous with the vesical venous plexus which drains into the internal iliac veins. The prostatic nerve plexus arises from the inferior hypogastric plexus; sympathetic fibers stimulate emission and glandular secretion of the prostate into the prostatic urethra.

The Cowper glands (bulbourethral) are pea-shaped mucous glands that insert into the spongy urethra below the external urethral sphincter. They are in the deep perineal pouch and help lubricate/deacidify the urethra with alkaline mucous.

The spermatic cord consists of three layers of fascia that arise from the layers of the abdominal wall; the external spermatic fascia comes from the external oblique, the cremasteric fascia/muscle from the internal oblique, and the internal spermatic fascia from the fascia transversalis. Note: the transversus abdominis is missing. The function of the spermatic cord is to attach the vessels of the testes to the testes themselves. The contents of the spermatic cord are the vas deferens, testicular artery, artery to ductus deferens (branch of the inferior vesicular artery), cremasteric artery (branch of the inferior epigastric artery), the pampiniform plexus (testicular veins), the genital branch of the genitofemoral nerve (causes the constriction of the cremasteric muscle), and the lymph vessels that extend to the para-aortic nodes.

The scrotum, or ballsack, separates the testes with a scrotal septum. The anterior portion of the scrotum is innervated by the ilioinguinal nerve (L1) and the posterior portion by the pudendal nerve (S2-S4). Superficial inguinal lymph nodes drain fluid from the scrotum.

Embryology

The urinary and genital systems share a common embryological origin from the intermediate mesoderm and cloaca. The intermediate mesoderm is located on the posterior abdominal wall and the cloaca forms from the terminal part of the hindgut. Intermediate mesoderm forms the urogenital ridge, which gives rise to both gonads and the urinary system.

The nephrogenic cord (NC) originates from intermediate mesoderm and extends from the cervical to the sacral portion of the embryo. The NC develops into the pronephros, the mesonephros (Wolffian duct), and the metanephros (permanent kidney). The pronephric duct is the initial kidney that eventually degenerates and forms the mesonephric duct. The mesonephric duct gives rise to the ureteric bud and male reproductive ducts. The metanephros becomes the permanent kidney, and develops from the ureteric bud of the mesonephros and the metanephric cap (aka metanephric blastema) from the nephrogenic cord. These two form the collecting system and the nephrons, respectively. To simplify, the ureteric bud forms the collecting part (collecting duct, calyxes, renal pelvis, ureter) of the kidney, and the metanephric blastema (cap) forms the excretory (nephron) part.

The cloaca is the terminal part of the hindgut shared by the urinary and digestive systems. The urorectal septum splits the cloaca into the urogenital sinus and the anorectal canal around week 4 of embryological development. The urogenital sinus is split into three parts: the upper part forms most of the bladder, the middle forms the urethra in females and the prostatic and membranous urethra in males, and the lower (phallic) part forms the male penile urethra and the female vestibule of the vagina. The male and female external genitalia originate from migratory mesoderm that develops around the cloacal membrane.

The genital tubercle elongates to form penis and clitoris, the urogenital (urethral) folds fuse to form the penile urethra and remain unfused to form labia minora, and the genital (labioscrotal) swellings fuse to form scrotum and remain unfused to form labia majora. Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) drives external genitalia masculinization at around week 7.

The Mesonephric (Wolffian duct) duct gives rise to the ureteric bud and the trigone in both males and females. It also forms the internal male reproductive structures (epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, and ejaculatory ducts) and female remnant structures (epoophoron, paroophoron, and Gartner's duct). The prostate and bulbourethral glands arise from urogenital sinus, not from the mesonephric duct. The mesonephric duct incorporated into the posterior bladder wall forms the trigone.

During development, the metanephric kidneys initially develop in the pelvis and receive blood supply from common iliac arteries. The kidneys then ascend to the upper lumbar region (T12-L3) and receive new vascular branches from the abdominal aorta as their previous vessels normally regress. The ureters elongate as kidneys ascend. Pelvic kidneys result as a failure of ascent. Horseshoe kidney (one large kidney with connected inferior poles) has its ascent arrested by the inferior mesenteric artery. Ureteric bud anomalies typically result in ureter obstruction. Double pelvis formation, bifid (split) ureter, ectopic ureter (may insert into vagina or urethra; continuous dribbling despite normal voiding), and ureter duplication are all forms of ureteric bud anomalies.

Some important congenital kidney anomalies to note are Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD), Autosomal Recessive Kidney disease, and Renal Dysgenesis. ADPKD is due to a mutation of the PKD1 gene on chromosome 16. It typically presents in young adults with associated berry aneurysm and hepatic cysts. ARPKD on the other hand presents in infancy with concurrent collection duct dilation and oligohydramnios due to impaired kidney filtering. Renal dysgenesis, or the improper development of the kidneys, is typically due to Potter sequence. Potter sequence presents with bilateral renal agenesis that causes the infant to be unable to produce/excrete amniotic fluid as urine, causing oligohydramnios and a concurrent slew of abnormalities (low set ears, beaked nose, downwards-slanting eyes, retrognathia, etc.).

Glomerular Disease

Understanding the nephrotic and nephritic syndromes of the kidney is necessary for Step 1. The necessary nephritic diseases lie under the scope of acute proliferative glomerulonephritis and rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN); the RPGN subtypes include anti-glomerular basement membrane (anti-GBM) RPGN, immune complex-mediated RPGN, IgA-associated RPGN, and pauci-immune RPGN. Other important glomerular conditions include diabetic glomerulosclerosis, lupus nephritis, and renal amyloidosis. Additional nephrotic glomerular diseases include minimal change disease, focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), membranous nephropathy, and membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN). These disorders each have different pathological patterns of glomerular injury that can lead to proteinuria, nephrotic syndrome, or progressive kidney dysfunction.

The foot processes of the podocyte slit diaphragm form bridges of proteins called nephrin molecules in order to facilitate selective filtration. Actin filaments and the protein CD2AP anchor podocin in place, which then anchors the nephrin molecules that form the extracellular bridges for the filtration process. Podocytes are forever cells like neurons; they do not regenerate. Loss of the podocyte slits causes proteinuria.

The glomerular basement membrane (GBM) is composed of heparin sulfate, laminin, nidogen, and collagen IV. The mesangium is composed of mesangial cells (smooth muscle-like) and is responsible for structural support and macrophage-like activity at the GBM; its ECM is composed of laminin, fibronectin, and collagen IV.

A pathological response to glomerular injury includes hypercellularity (proliferation of the mesangial cells, leukocyte infiltration, endocapillary proliferation, crescent formation), GBM thickening (positive on PAS, thickening of capillary wall on histology, increase of GBM proteins, increased dense material on electron microscopy), hyalinosis of the glomeruli, and sclerosis.

Classification of glomerular injury includes distributional classification, part of glomeruli afflicted, immunofluorescence pattern, and pathogenesis. Glomerular diseases can be either diffuse (affects all the glomeruli) or focal (involves only part of the glomerulus). Diseases can also be global (affecting all parts of the glomerulus) or segmental (affects only a part of the glomeruli, for instance: only the mesangium, or only the capillaries are afflicted). Immunofluorescence stainings reveal if a disease has a granular pattern (which signals immune complex deposition) or a linear pattern (which signals an observed anti-GBM disease).

The pathogenesis of glomerular diseases are specific to each type of disease. For example, subepithelial humps are characteristic of acute glomerulonephritis. Membranous deposits are membranous nephropathy, subendothelial deposits are membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis, and mesangial deposits are IgA nephropathy.

Acute kidney injury (AKI) causes a rapid decrease in glomerular filtration. It is characterized by an abrupt uptick in serum BUN and creatinine, with subsequent disruption of salt and water metabolism.

Nephritic Syndrome

Nephritic syndrome is the pathological inflammation of the glomeruli. Common findings include proliferation of cells, inflammatory cells, severe injury to glomerular capillaries, and bleeding into the glomerulus. Clinical findings include hematuria, proteinuria, reduced GFR and subsequent oliguria with uremia, and hypertension with secondary renin release. Symptoms involve flu-like symptoms due to aforementioned inflammation and uremia, peripheral edema, eye puffiness, and increased blood pressure. Urinalysis reveals a cola-colored hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs and acanthocytes, proteinuria, and azotemia (increased BUN and creatinine). Red blood casts are also visible in microscopy.

A RBC cast found in the urine is a microscopic urine sediment found in pathological nephritic diseases. RBCs casts are red-brown and do not require staining to visualize; they are made from RBCs and WBCs molded together.

Acute Proliferative Glomerulonephritis (APG)

Acute Proliferative Glomerulonephritis (APG), also known as acute poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis, often occurs 1-4 weeks after group A streptococcus (GAS) infection. In rare cases, lupus nephritis can also cause APG. It is an acute nephritic syndrome with additional possible nephrotic characteristics. Lab tests show decreased serum complement proteins due to deposits of complement in the GBM. Serum anti-streptolysin O antibody is also found in cases where APG is caused by GAS. Most kids (95%) who get this disease are eventually fine, but only 1/3 of adults are able to recover. In severe cases, this disease can progress to rapidly progressive glomerulosclerosis.

APG shows the diffuse proliferation of glomerular cells and WBCs. Immune complexes (IgG and complement proteins) form deposits in the GBM. Global involvement of these subepithelial (between GBM and podocytes) depositions causes enlarged, hypercellular glomeruli on microscopy with markedly increased inflammatory cells and less visible capillaries. On electron microscopy, characteristic "subepithelial humps" are visible. On IF, a granular pattern with IgG and C3 complement staining is visible.

Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

RPG is also known as Crescentic glomerulonephritis. It is antibody mediated nephritic syndrome with acute renal failure, severe oliguria, and proteinuria. The percentage of the glomeruli with crescents is used in determining prognosis, with less than 80% of the total glomeruli having crescents being favorable. Leaving RPGN untreated leads to renal failure and RPGN is treated with immunosuppression and plasma exchange.

This severe glomerular injury is multifactorial with hematuria, RBC casts in urine, moderate proteinuria, enlarged kidneys. In histology, focal and segmental necrosis can be visualized. Either diffuse or focal epithelial mesangial proliferation can possibly occur. The characteristic crescents can be visualized within the glomeruli and have prominent fibrin. Macrophages and monocytes are also present. Electron microscopy reveals wrinkling and ruptures in the basement membrane that leads to the leakage of fibrin and debris into Bowman's space. In IF, a linear GBM pattern is seen in Goodpasture syndrome, when antibodies attack the basement membrane of both the kidneys and the lungs. It is also key to note that granular deposits of RPGN visualized in IF are due to idiopathic causes, acute proliferative glomerulonephritis, Lupus nephritis, Henoch-Schoenlein purpura, and IgA nephropathy (Berger disease).

IgA nephropathy (Berger disease) is the most common primary RPGN, and presents with prominent IgA deposits in the mesangium, causing mesangial proliferation and matrix increase. Henoch-Schoenlein purpura (HSP) is a childhood syndrome that causes purpuric skin lesions, abdominal pain, intestinal bleeding, and arthralgias. Renal abnormalities and IgA deposits as seen in Berger disease are also present. HSP is essentially a worse Berger.

Pauci-immune RPGN has no immune deposits to visualize in IF and is highly associated with Anti-neutrophil circulating antibodies (ANCA). ANCA has c-ANCA (cytoplasmic) and p-ANCA (perinuclear) subtypes. Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA) presents with elevated c-ANCA, Microscopic polyangiitis (MPA) with p-ANCA, and Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA) with p-ANCA. These three lead to elevated ANCA which causes Pauci-immune RPGN. Pauci immune RPGN also presents with characteristic crescents and a silver stain can be used to visualize the loss of GBM.

Nephrotic Syndromes

Nephrotic syndromes are distinct from nephritic syndromes, as instead of glomerular inflammation nephrotic syndromes are characterized by heavy protein loss via urine due to podocyte injury. Common findings include massive proteinuria (>3.5 grams of protein in urine per day), hypoalbuminemia, generalized edema, hyperlipidemia, and lipiduria. Common causes of nephrotic syndromes are Diabetes, Amyloidosis, and Systemic Lupus erythematosus. Other causes include NSAIDs, malaria, syphilus, hep B, hep C, HIV, and malignancy.

Important nephrotic syndromes to know are minimal change disease, membranous nephropathy, focal segmental glomerulosclerosis, membronoproliferative glomerulonephritis, and sometimes IgA nephropathy.

Diabetes mellitus damages the glomerulus via nodular glomerulosclerosis. Ball-like matrix lesions are characteristic of nephrotic syndrome secondary to diabetes. Diffuse mesangial sclerosis, diffuse nodular sclerosis, and mesangial matrix proliferation are all seen in the glomeruli as well. Moreover, acellular nodules, GBM thickening, and renal arteriosclerosis are all secondary to diabetes.

Lupus nephritis can cause both nephritic and nephrotic symptoms. The pathological process of nephrosis due to Lupus are similar to what has previously been discussed; mesangial cell and matrix proliferation, crescent formation, granular IF pattern, and advanced sclerosing are all present. One characteristic development from Lupus is the thickening of capillary walls with a "wire-loop" appearance. The "wire-loops" are the thickening of peripheral capillary loops due to immune deposits.

Amyloidosis is the deposition of misfolded protein in the glomeruli. It can be visualized as "apple-green" birefringence on a Congo Red Stain. Amyloidosis would present with abnormalities in various other organs in the body.

Minimal Change Disease

Minimal change disease is a nephrotic syndrome that occurs due to the effacement of podocyte processes. It is most frequent in pediatric (2-6 years old) cases and has a generally good prognosis. Minimal change disease presents with massive proteinuria, no hypertension, no hematuria, and normal renal output volume. It is treated with steroids.

Minimal change disease is a glomerular disease with an absence of immune deposits. Instead, the immune system targets and eliminates the podocyte slit diaphragm. In histology, glomeruli appear completely normal with a PAS stain. Nothing notable is shown on IF. Electron microscopy shows uniform and diffuse effacement of the podocytes.

Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

FSGS is the most common cause of adult nephrotic syndrome. In FSGS, only a select few glomeruli are affected, and only a specific segment of the glomerular tuft is afflicted (not diffuse throughout the glomeruli itself). Symptoms and signs include those associated with nephrotic syndrome, hypertension, slight hematuria, and azotemia (high nitrogen-containing compounds in the blood).

Most cases of FSGS are idiopathic. There is a specific subtype of FSGS associated with a collapse of the glomerular tuft visible on light microscopy that is associated with HIV-associated nephropathy and poor prognosis. FSGS is also associated with heroin usage, sickle-cell, and morbid obesity. FSGS can also occur secondary to IgA nephropathy and focal glomerulonephritis.

FSGS presents with hyalinosis and sclerosis of the mesangium. Also the effacement of podocyte foot processes, with concurrent IgM and C3 depositions in the mesangium. Due to the hyalinosis of the mesangium, half of the glomerulus looks thicker than the other. Note: this is not crescent formation, as seen in RPGN. The capillaries hardly have any visible lumen left. On EM, the effacement of foot processes can be clearly seen along with GBM thickening.

Membranous Nephropathy

MN is due to the diffuser thickening of the glomerular capillary wall. It occurs with subepithelial deposits of IgG secondary to drug-related toxicity (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, gold, penicillamine), carcinoma, mesothelioma, SLE, thyroiditis, Hep B, Hep C, HIV, syphilis, schistosomiasis, or even malaria. MN is an immune-complex mediated disease with autoantibodies to phospholipase A2 receptors causing membrane attack complex formation and deposition into the glomerulus.

Typically presents in patients 30-60 as a nephrotic syndrome with concurrent hematuria and hypertension. Has a slow onset with persistent proteinuria. Rarely causes ESRD. In MN's early stages, uniform, diffuse thickening of the capillary wall with dome and spike immune deposits can be visualized. EM reveals dense deposits GBM and epithelial cells with concurrent effacement of podocyte foot processes. On IF, there is a granular pattern due to those immune and complement complexes. Note: three things to remember for MN are thickened GBM, subepithelial deposits of IgG and C3, and effacement of foot processes; a key term to note for identifying this disease is the presence of "spike and dome" deposits.

Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis

MPGN has both nephrotic and nephritic symptoms as well as two subtypes. MPGN type I typically occurs in adolescents and young adults with hematuria. It progresses over 10 years until becoming RPGN with chronic renal failure. Treatment with steroids is often ineffective. MPGN II is also known as Dense Deposit disease; it primarily occurs in adults and is due to chronic immune disorders: SLE, HBV, HCV, HIV, endocarditis, schistosomiasis, A1AT deficiency, and lymphoid tumors.

On light microscopy, MPGN can be seen with proliferation of the mesangium/matrix, loss of capillary lumen, and thickening of the GBM. Dense deposits are seen on EM in the mesangium and the GBM; the subendothelial deposits are originally between the subendothelium and the GBM, but as the GBM thickens it engulfs the deposits entirely. Pathological endothelial and mesangial proliferation is present in MPGN. The lobular, proliferative pattern is commonly referred to as a "tram-track" appearance on light microscopy.

Dense deposit disease (type II) is characterized by excessive activation of the alternate complement pathway. C3 nephritic factor is an autoantibody that stabilizes complement and continually activates the pathway; it is commonly seen in those with DDD and is responsible for the accumulation of complement deposits in the glomerulus. It is commonly seen in children and young adults and has a poor prognosis (half of all cases progress to ESRD). On EM, a dense ribbon can be seen in the basement membrane. On IF, no deposits can be seen in the thick ribbon, but there are C3 deposits in the mesangium.

Tubulointerstitial Diseases

Azotemia is the biological evidence of kidney dysfunction due to elevated levels of nitrogen waste products (BUN and CR) found in blood labs. It is asymptomatic. Uremia is a symptomatic disease that occurs when nitrogen waste products build up in the blood due to severe kidney dysfunction and causes multisystem organ dysfunction. Azotremia is the precursor to uremia.

Causes of azotemia can be pre-renal, renal, or post renal. "Pre-renal" covers instances of inadequate blood flow to the kidneys. "Renal" is due to direct injury to parts of the renal cortex or medulla, and involves tubular cell injury. "Postrenal" is due to urinary obstruction, typically due to kidney stones or urinary tract infection.

Pre-renal dysfunction is due to either a primary cardiac problem that causes decreased cardiac output, a decrease in intravascular volume, or dysfunction of the renal artery (coarctation or stenosis). In cases of pre-renal dysfunction, a FeNa <1% is the classical finding to confirm the disease is, in fact, pre-renal in origin.

RAAS review: Renin is released from juxtaglomerular cells due to low volume/low Na+ detected by the macula densa cells of the DCT. Sympathetic stimulation of Beta-1 receptors can also trigger juxtaglomerular cell stimulation, increasing the release of renin. Moreover, macula densa cells can release prostaglandins to vasodilate the afferent and efferent arterioles to promote increased GFR.

There are two main pathological processes of tubulointerstitial diseases: Acute tubular injury (ATI) with/without acute tubular necrosis (ATN) and interstitial nephritis. Causes of ATI are ischemia, nephrotoxins, or a combination of the two. Interstitial nephritis involves inflammation of the glomerular interstitium, and can be drug/toxin induced or infection induced (pyelonephritis).

Types of ATN

Ischemic causes of ATN can be due to either hypoperfusion (typical causes of this being trauma or shock) or another disruption of the renal circulation. Nephrotoxic causes of ATN can be either endogenous or exogenous; endogenous referring to toxic agents from substances released by the body due to cellular injury, and exogenous referring to external toxic agents (for instance, mercury). Since the cells of the PCT have a high metabolic rate, they are very sensitive to ischemia and toxins.

Pathogenesis of Ischemia and Functional Defects

The pathogenesis of ischemia includes decreased glomerular blood flow that causes endothelial injury, which triggers vasoconstrictors (endothelin) and downregulates vasodilators (NO2). A decrease in renal perfusion also triggers RAAS. Failure of the PCT to reabsorb sodium causes increased sodium delivery to the DCT. This causes reflexive vasoconstriction and decreased GFR. Further obstruction of renal vasculature, due to debris and inflammation, causes decreased GFR as well.

Tubulointerstitial Nephritis (TN)

The absence of a nephritic or nephrotic syndrome with impaired renal function implies that tubulointerstitial nephritis may be the main suspect. TN presents with defects in tubular function, inflammatory markers, and can be either acute or chronic. Acute TN can be seen with interstitial edema, leukocyte infiltration of the interstitium and tubules, and tubular injury. Chronic TN can further present with monocyte infiltration, interstitial fibrosis, and tubular atrophy.

Causes of TN include: pyelonephritis, radiation, chemicals/drugs/toxins, metabolic dysfunction, ischemia, and immune dysfunction. The three main culprits of renal drug toxicity are vancomycin, gentamicin, and amphotericin. Vancomycin inhibits cell wall synthesis of gram positive bacteria; its cause of toxicity is still unknown. Gentamicin is part of the family of aminoglycoside drugs and is a bactericidal that binds to the 30S ribosomal unit to combat gram negative bacteria. It is directly toxic to tubular cells (especially PCT) and requires close monitoring when administered. Amphotericin is a drug that binds to the fungal cell membrane and is used to treat fungal infections. It is also directly toxic to tubular cells and can also cause afferent arteriole constriction. Amphotericin is less toxic when prepared in liposomal formulation.

Gadolinium is an element that can be used for MRI imaging. In patients with renal disease, it is highly nephrotoxic and can lead to nephrogenic fibrosis. The element forms deposits in skin and organs. Cyclosporine and Tacrolimus are immunosuppressants and calcineurin inhibitors. They constrict the afferent arteriole and have direct nephrotoxic effects on tubules long term, eventually causing glomerulosclerosis and interstitial fibrosis. Drugs against Hep B and HIV (reverse transcriptase inhibitors such as tenofovir) can also cause renal toxicity and, classically, Fanconi syndrome. Lithium is a treatment of manic depressive disorder and can also reduce aquaporin expression via nephrogenic diabetes insipidus. Long term, this can cause chronic kidney disease.

Both hemoglobin and myoglobin can cause renal injury. Myoglobin is released into the bloodstream due to rhabdomyolysis, and hemoglobinuria can be caused in transfusion reactions or lysing of RBCs. The iron within these molecules can cause nephron toxicity, and the protein itself can cause tubular obstruction. Rhabdomyalisis can cause dark urine that tests positive for heme. It can also cause elevated CK levels in serum.

It is also important to note that just about any drug can cause a hypersensitivity reaction that injures the kidneys, and some drugs do not even need to cause a hypersensitivity reaction to cause kidney injury. Findings of a type I HSN are fever, rash, eosinophilia, marked increased IgE, and eosinophils in urine sediment. Mild hematuria, proteinuria, oliguria, and elevated serum creatinine are also present in renal dysfunction related to HSN.

Vascular and Cystic Diseases of the Kidney

Renal artery narrowing (renal artery stenosis) is most commonly caused by atherosclerotic plaque, which accounts for about 70-90% of cases and typically affects older adults, especially at the proximal renal artery near the aorta. The second most common cause is fibromuscular dysplasia (about 10-25%), a non-atherosclerotic vascular disorder usually seen in younger women that produces a characteristic "string-of-beads" appearance in the mid-distal renal artery. Arteritis, such as Takayasu arteritis, is a rare cause and results from inflammatory damage to the arterial wall. All of these conditions can reduce renal perfusion, activating the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and leading to secondary hypertension, and eventual ischemia. In cases of renal ischemia, the affected kidney is shrunken with interstitial fibrosis and focal inflammatory infiltrate. The contralateral kidney has hyaline arteriosclerosis due to hypertension (from increased blood flow due to more filtration demand).

Fibromuscular dysplasia has an unknown etiology and is characterized by medial fibrosis without inflammation or atherosclerosis. Typical demographic of young women. It is diagnosed with a string of beads sign on angiogram, and can only be treated with angioplasty.

Takayasu arteritis is a large-vessel vasculitis that primarily affects the aorta and its major branches, leading to inflammation and narrowing of these arteries. The disease is characterized by granulomatous inflammation of the vessel wall with intimal thickening, which can cause stenosis and ischemia. It is most commonly present in young women of Asian descent. Blood pressure differences between arms, and systemic inflammatory symptoms. Treatment typically involves corticosteroids to suppress the inflammatory process, with additional immunosuppressive agents used in refractory cases.

Renal vein thrombosis occurs when a clot forms in the renal vein, most commonly due to a hypercoagulable state (which can be associated with nephrotic syndrome), particularly from loss of anticoagulant proteins (antithrombin III) in the urine. Other causes include extrinsic compression of the renal vein or malignant growth. Patients present with flank pain, hematuria, and worsening renal function. Diagnosis is typically made with Doppler ultrasound or CT imaging, and treatment generally involves anticoagulation, with thrombolytic therapy considered in severe or acute cases.

Sickle cell nephropathy occurs in sickle cell disease due to increased sickling of red blood cells within the renal medulla, which has a hyperosmolar, hypoxic, and acidic environment that promotes HbS polymerization. This leads to microvascular thrombosis, causing ischemic injury to the kidney. Patients may present with hematuria, mild proteinuria, and patchy renal papillary necrosis.

Renal infarction due to embolism occurs when an embolus blocks blood flow in a renal artery or one of its branches, leading to ischemic tissue death. Common sources include fatty or cholesterol emboli (which occur during manipulation of the aorta), mural thrombi (form in the left atrium or ventricle after a myocardial infarction), and the septic emboli that originate from infected cardiac valves in infective endocarditis. These emboli lodge in renal vessels and cause sudden flank pain, hematuria, and impaired kidney function. On gross pathology, a wedge shape, well demarcated, outer foci of hemorrhage is visible. Ischemic coagulative necrosis is visible on light microscopy.

Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) is a medium-vessel vasculitis that can cause renal infarction due to inflammation and damage of renal arteries. The disease leads to segmental arterial inflammation with microaneurysm formation, which can rupture or promote thrombosis and downstream ischemia. PAN commonly affects the renal, heart, gastrointestinal tract, and skin, but classically spares the lungs. Imaging often shows a "string-of-beads" (similar to fibromuscular dysplasia) appearance due to alternating aneurysms and stenosis in affected arteries. Treatment involves immunosuppressive therapy, typically corticosteroids with additional agents such as cyclophosphamide in severe cases.

Thrombotic Microangiopathy occurs when thrombus formation in arterioles/capillary leads to ischemia. Impeded blood flow causes the shearing of RBCs, leading to microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA). The consumption of platelets leads to thrombocytopenia with additional neurological manifestations.

Hemolytic uremic syndrome can be typical (endothelial injury due to E. Coli, Shiga-like toxins) or atypical (inherited abnormalities in regulatory complement proteins or malignant hypertension). Childhood HUS is one of the main causes of acute renal failure in children. HUS presents with a flu-like prodrome and can cause severe oliguria, hematuria, neurological changes and microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA).

Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura is caused by a deficiency in the ADAMTS13 enzyme that causes increased platelet aggregation. Typically presents in young women with fever, neurological symptoms, MAHA, thrombocytopenia, and renal failure. It is treated with plasmapheresis.

Nephrosclerosis is thought to be caused by hypertension and is the hyaline arteriosclerosis of the renal arterioles and small arteries. Presents with thickened intima and media with loss of function in nephrons. Presents with hyalinization of the glomerulus (glomerulosclerosis), interstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy, and a leathery appearance of the capsule of the kidney. Can develop into HUS and MAHA as a result of endothelial injury.

Cystic Diseases

Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease

ADPKD presents in young adults with bilateral cysts on the renal parenchyma. Typically results in renal failure around 40-50 and can lead to end stage renal disease (ESRD). 85% of cases are caused by mutations in the PKD1 gene on chromosome 16, and 15% are due to mutations in PKD2 on chromosome 4. Presents as renal insufficiency, hemorrhage, growing cysts, palpable on abdominal exam, onset of hematuria, and symptoms of chronic kidney diseases (proteinuria, polyuria, hypertension). Can also develop cysts in the liver and intracranial berry aneurysms in the circle of willis (subarachnoid hemorrhages). Also causes Mitral Valve Prolapse and other cardiovascular abnormalities.

Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease

ARPKD occurs in infants. One-fourth of infants die due to pulmonary hypoplasia caused by oligohydramnios associated with this disease. Survivors present with renal insufficiency, hepatic fibrosis, and portal hypertension. A mutation in the PKHD1 gene on Chromosome 6 causes fibrocystin to mutate, causing improper cell differentiation in the kidneys, liver, and pancreas. Kidneys enlarge with a smooth external appearance. Numerous small cysts in the cortex and medulla give the kidney a spongy appearance, and dilation of the collecting tubules is also seen.

Other Cystic Kidney Diseases

Multicystic Dysplasia / Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney (MCDK) is a developmental disorder in which the kidney fails to properly develop and instead becomes a mass of (usually unilateral but can be bilateral) unfunctional cysts. Occurs in infants, typically asymptomatic if unilateral presentation, and is identified with abdominal palpation and ultrasound.

Acquired Cystic Disease occurs as a result of ESRD in patients with prolonged dialysis. Cysts measure 0.1-4 cm in diameter and are filled with clear fluid. They form as a result of obstruction of the renal tubules or due to the presence of oxalate crystals. The cysts can bleed and increase risk of renal cell carcinoma.

Obstructive Renal Diseases

Obstructive renal disease (postrenal AKI) results from blockage of urine flow anywhere from the renal pelvis to the urethra, leading to increased intratubular pressure, reduced GFR, and kidney injury. Common causes depend on location and age, including nephrolithiasis, tumors, and prostatic enlargement (BPH). Clinically, patients may have flank pain, urinary retention, or decreased urine output, and prolonged obstruction can cause hydronephrosis and permanent renal damage. Diagnosis is supported by elevated serum creatinine and imaging (Noncontrast CT, ultrasound) showing urinary tract dilation and possibly the obstruction. Management requires urgent relief of the obstruction (catheterization, stenting), as renal function often improves if treated early.

Acute Urate Nephropathy

AUN is the deposition of uric acid crystals in the renal tubules, leading to intrarenal obstruction. Chemotherapy is a major risk factor for AUN. This disease presents as AKI (increased creatinine) and oliguria shortly after chemo, with massive amounts of uric acid seen in serum. Acidic urine is also characteristic with the presence of uric acid kidney stones. Kidneys appear normal on imaging. Note: AKI after chemo with hyperuricemia is a major indicator of AUN.

Chronic Urate Nephropathy

CUN is the chronic, pathological buildup of urate in the renal interstitium that leads to chronic inflammation and CKD. Typically due to gout or lead exposure. Characterized by slow elevation of creatinine levels in serum and mild proteinuria. In histology, giant cells can be seen in the medulla of the kidneys. The kidneys themselves are small and scarred.

Nephrocalcinosis

Metastatic calcification due to elevated levels of serum Ca++ leads to nephrocalcinosis, which is the deposition of calcium salts in the renal tissue. High Ca++ can be due to hyperparathyroidism or distal RTA. This disease causes polyuria, kidney stones, and CKD. Alkaline urine is characteristic as well as calcium deposits in the tubular basement membrane and interstitium. Diffuse renal calcification is the key phrase for this disease.

Obstructive Uropathy

OU is caused by a urinary flow obstruction that leads to increased tubular hydrostatic pressure and decreased GFR. Can be caused by BPH, kidney stones, tumors, or anything that can cause an obstruction in the urinary tract. Presents with flank pain, urinary retention, hydronephrosis, AKI, elevated creatinine, and possible hematuria. Causes interstitial fibrosis and tubular dilation. Dilated renal pelvis on imaging is very characteristic of this disease.

Kidney Stones

Kidney stones are otherwise known as nephrolithiasis, renal calculi, or urolithiasis are a cause of urinary tract obstruction. Nephrolithiasis/renal calculi refers to the formation of a stone mass in the renal system, whereas urolithiasis refers to the formation of a stone in the urinary tract or bladder. All present with unilateral flank pain that radiates to the groin and with hematuria. Kidney stones are detected with noncontrast CT, as only some are visible by X-ray. There are 5 main types of nephrolithiasis to know for USMLE Step 1.

Calcium Oxalate

Calcium Oxalate stones are the most common type of nephrolithiasis in adults and occurs due to the precipitation of calcium-oxalate. They are caused by ethylene glycol (antifreeze), Crohn's disease, or dehydration and appear as dumbbell or envelope shaped crystals. Patients with this type of kidney stone can have elevated calcium, but they can also form with present normocalcemia. Urine pH is variable. They appear radiopaque (visible) on noncontrast CT and cause renal fibrosis with tubular and pelvic dilation (hydronephrosis).

Calcium Phosphate

Calcium Phosphate crystals have a spiny, wedge, crystalline appearance and precipitate in alkaline urine. Hyperparathyroidism and distal RTA (type 1) can cause these stones. Elevated serum calcium is typically (but not always) seen. Appears radiopaque on CT.

Struvite Stones

Struvite stones, or Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate crystals, are caused by urease positive bacteria that convert urea into ammonia and alkalize urine. Proteus, Klebsiella, and other UTI suspects can cause these types of stones. They appear concurrently with UTIs and appear as coffin lid crystals. They also have the potential to form a staghorn calculi in the renal pelvis, which is a huge stone that requires surgical removal. Urease positive UTI is the number one sign that a kidney stone is a struvite.

Uric Acid Stone

Uric acid in the urine leads to low urine pH and the possibility of precipitating uric acid kidney stones. Gout, Leukemia, and chronic diarrhea all cause high urine concentrations of uric acid. Colicky flank pain and hematuria cause these rhomboid crystals to form. They are radiolucent on X-ray (very hard to spot) and visible on CT scan. Note: uric acid in urine, low urine pH, and invisible on X-ray are all highly notable for uric acid stones.

Cystine Stones

Classically found in children, cystine stones are hexagonal in appearance and occur due to cystinuria which is due to defective COLA (cystine, ornithine, lysine, arginine) reabsorption in the PCT. They are largely due to genetics and occur recurrently in young patients. A positive cyanide-nitroprusside test indicates a positive for cystinuria. Urine in cystinuria is acidic (low pH). These crystals are also faintly radiopaque (difficult to see but still more visible than radiolucent). Note: although these crystals are mostly found in children, the most common kidney stones in youth are calcium phosphate crystals.

Pyelonephritis and Cystitis

Acute Cystitis

Acute cystitis is a lower urinary tract infection (bladder) that is typically caused by the same microbes that cause urinary tract infections (UTI). The most common causal organism is uropathogenic E. coli (80% of cases) since E. coli is native to the nearby gut microbiome and (in females primarily) can ascend the urethra/stick to the bladder with type 1 fimbriae and P pili. This allows colonization of the bladder without systemic invasion. It is especially common in sexually active women.

Urinalysis typically shows pyuria and bacteriuria, with positive leukocyte esterase and sometimes nitrites (depending on the causal organism). Clinically, patients present with dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, and suprapubic pain, but fever and other systemic symptoms are usually absent (distinguishing it from pyelonephritis). There are no WBC casts, as infection is limited to the bladder.

Acute Pyelonephritis

Acute pyelonephritis is the infection of the kidney due to an infection of the urinary tract that ascends (due to vesicoureteral reflux) and colonizes in the renal pelvis, tubules, and parenchyma. This causes tubulointerstitial inflammation. The most common pathogen remains E. coli, but other gram-negative organisms may also be involved.

Histologically (and on urinalysis), this condition is characterized by WBC casts and neutrophilic infiltration of renal tubules, indicating kidney involvement. Clinically, patients present with high fever, chills, flank pain, and costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness, often with systemic symptoms such as fever. Note: distinguish pyelonephritis from cystitis by presence of fever and WBC casts.

Catheter-Associated UTI

CAUTI is a UTI caused by an infected catheter (this is an example of a fomite, which is an inanimate object that can carry infectious bacteria) inserted into the bladder. Bacteria form biofilms on catheter surfaces, leading to persistent and often polymicrobial infections. Common organisms include E. coli, Enterococcus, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella, and Proteus.

Urinalysis often shows polymicrobial bacteriuria, and infections may be harder to treat due to biofilm resistance. Proteus species are notable for producing urease, which alkalinizes urine and promotes stone formation; they also exhibit swarming motility (increasing virulence). Clinically, these infections occur in hospitalized or catheterized patients, and rare findings like purple urine bag syndrome (due to bacterial metabolism turning urine indigo) may be seen.

Chronic Pyelonephritis

Chronic pyelonephritis results from recurrent or persistent kidney infections, often due to vesicoureteral reflux or chronic obstruction, leading to recurrent cycles of inflammation and healing. Over time, this causes interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy, progressively impairing renal function.

Characteristic findings include "thyroidization" of tubules (tubules filled with eosinophilic casts resembling thyroid follicles) and polar cortical scarring, especially at the upper and lower poles in reflux-associated disease. Clinically, patients may develop chronic kidney disease, and imaging shows small, asymmetric kidneys. Note: "thyroidization" of the tubules and eosinophilic casts are major buzzwords for this disease.

Interstitial Cystitis

Interstitial cystitis is a noninfectious chronic bladder condition caused by disruption of the glycosaminoglycan (GAG) layer, which causes increased bladder permeability. This allows potassium to penetrate and irritate subepithelial nerves, resulting in chronic pain and type I hypersensitivity. Cystoscopy may reveal Hunner ulcers and glomerulations (petechial bladder hemorrhages). Unlike infectious cystitis, urine cultures are sterile. Patients present with chronic pelvic/bladder pain, urinary frequency, and urgency, with pain often relieved by voiding. Note: pain relief upon urination is a key distinguishing feature.

Gonococcal Urethritis

Gonococcal urethritis is a sexually transmitted disease that is caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae; this intracellular gram-negative pathogen has pili that it can use to attach to the urethra. It also has a special protein called IgA protease that it can use to eliminate IgA antibodies. Note: this infection primarily affects the urethra, mainly in sexually active individuals.

Diagnosis is supported by finding intracellular gram-negative diplococci within neutrophils on microscopy. Clinically, patients present with dysuria and purulent urethral discharge, often with increased WBCs in the first-void urine sample. It is important to treat promptly due to risk of complications and coinfection with Chlamydia.

High-Yield Microbes

Escherichia coli

Escherichia coli, especially uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC), is the most common cause of UTIs. UPEC comes from normal gut flora and causes infection by ascending from the urethra into the bladder and sometimes up to the kidney, producing cystitis and pyelonephritis. Its key virulence factor in renal disease is the P fimbria (pyelonephritis-associated pili), which binds specifically to urogenital epithelium and promotes adherence, making ascent and persistence possible. On basic micro, E. coli is a gram-negative bacillus, lactose fermenter, forms pink colonies on MacConkey agar (and green on eosin-methylene blue), has hemolysin, and has the gram-negative characteristic LPS endotoxin (which is important in severe infection and urosepsis).

Clinically, E. coli causes lower UTI with dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, pyuria, and bacteriuria. It also causes pyelonephritis with fever, flank pain, and CVA tenderness. In the kidney, acute pyelonephritis causes neutrophilic infiltration of the tubules and interstitium, usually sparing glomeruli and vessels. For renal block exams, the key association is: E. coli = ascending UTI pathogen, not immune-complex glomerulonephritis. A separate high-yield subtype, EHEC, matters because its Shiga toxin can trigger HUS, which causes renal injury by a different mechanism than classic UPEC.

Streptococcus spp.

Streptococcus species, particularly group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes, aka GAS), are most important in renal pathology due to their role in poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN). This occurs 2-4 weeks after pharyngitis or 1-6 weeks after skin infections (impetigo) and is caused by subepithelial immune complex deposition, leading to complement activation and glomerular inflammation. Clinically, PSGN presents as a nephritic syndrome with hematuria (cola-colored urine), mild proteinuria, edema, and hypertension, along with low complement levels and elevated ASO or anti-DNase B titers indicating recent infection.

Beyond PSGN, Streptococcus species are less common causes of UTIs compared to gram-negative bacteria. The key exam takeaway is that Strep = immune-mediated kidney disease, not direct infection of the kidney in most cases. Management of PSGN is supportive (control BP and edema), with antibiotics only if there is active infection, and prognosis is generally good in children but worse in adults.

Enterococcus spp.

Enterococcus species (E. faecalis and E. faecium) are gram-positive, catalase-negative cocci that normally reside in the gastrointestinal tract and are important renal pathogens mainly because they cause UTIs, especially after GI/GU procedures and in hospitalized or catheterized patients. Microbiologically, they can appear in pairs or chains, are PYR positive, and are notable for their ability to grow in bile and 6.5% NaCl, reflecting their resistance as microbes. For exam purposes, the big clue is that enterococci are more resistant than typical streptococci and have intrinsic resistance to cephalosporins, penicillin G, and vancomycin.

Clinically, Enterococcus causes complicated UTIs more often than simple community cystitis and should be thought of in patients with catheters, instrumentation, or healthcare-associated infection. It can also cause subacute endocarditis, which matters because renal findings in endocarditis can be secondary to infection-associated kidney injury. The key renal-board takeaway is: Enterococcus = GI flora, hardy nosocomial GU pathogen, complicated UTI organism, and resistance-heavy bug. Treatment is with linezolid and second-line tigecycline (would treat with vancomycin if the strain is not resistant).

Proteus mirabilis

Proteus mirabilis is a gram-negative enteric rod and an important renal pathogen because it commonly causes nosocomial (infections in healthcare places like hospitals) UTIs and is strongly associated with struvite stone formation. It is part of the normal gastrointestinal flora and is classically identified by swarming motility and urease production. The urease is the key renal virulence factor: it converts urea to ammonia, which alkalinizes the urine and promotes precipitation of magnesium ammonium phosphate (struvite) stones. These stones can become large staghorn calculi, especially in the setting of upper urinary tract infection or catheterization. For exams, the main association is: Proteus = UTI + alkaline urine + struvite stones. It is especially relevant in catheter-associated and healthcare-associated UTIs, where it may also contribute to catheter obstruction.

Klebsiella spp.

Klebsiella pneumoniae is a gram-negative, lactose-fermenting, encapsulated enteric rod that is an important renal pathogen because it causes UTIs, especially nosocomial/complicated UTIs, and is urease positive, which raises urine pH and promotes struvite stone formation. For renal exams, the key associations are hospital-acquired UTI, multidrug resistance, and alkaline urine with concurrent struvite stones/staghorn calculi.

Staphylococcus saprophyticus

Staphylococcus saprophyticus is a coagulase-negative, catalase-positive, novobiocin-resistant gram-positive coccus that is part of the normal female genital tract/perineal microbiome and is the second most common cause of UTI in sexually active women. For renal exams, the key associations are acute uncomplicated cystitis and urease production, which can cause alkaline urine and may contribute to struvite stones.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a gram-negative, aerobic, oxidase-positive, catalase-positive, nonlactose-fermenting rod that thrives in water and moist environments and is important in renal medicine mainly as a nosocomial UTI pathogen, especially in patients with catheters, instrumentation, or other complicated healthcare-associated UTIs. It is notable for intrinsic and acquired multidrug resistance and for virulence factors including a mucoid polysaccharide capsule that promotes biofilm formation, endotoxin, Exotoxin A, phospholipase C, and the pigments pyocyanin and pyoverdin; in culture it can produce blue-green pus and a sweet odor. For a renal block exam, the key association is: Pseudomonas = resistant hospital-acquired complicated UTI/urosepsis organism, not a classic community cystitis bug, and management depends on culture-guided therapy with antipseudomonal agents because resistance is common.

Neisseria gonorrhoeae

Neisseria gonorrhoeae is a gram-negative, aerobe that is often found intracellularly within neutrophils and is a key GU pathogen because it causes gonococcal urethritis with dysuria and purulent urethral discharge. It uses adhesins/pili to attach to epithelium and IgA1 protease to aid mucosal infection; Gram stain in symptomatic urethritis classically appears as intracellular gram-negative diplococci. For renal/GU exams, the big association is STI-related urethritis rather than cystitis, with first-void urine showing urethral inflammation, and diagnosis is best made by NAAT, often with concurrent testing for chlamydia.

Leptospira interrogans

Leptospira interrogans is a spirochete acquired from animal urine in contaminated water (classically rats) through broken skin or mucous membranes, and it matters for renal exams because severe leptospirosis (Weil disease) causes acute kidney injury with jaundice/liver failure. The high-yield renal takeaway is: exposure to contaminated freshwater or animal urine + AKI + jaundice = leptospirosis. It is a zoonotic spirochete, not a typical ascending UTI pathogen, and is treated with penicillin G and tetracyclines.

Tumors of the Kidney, Lower Urinary Tract, and Prostate

Kidney neoplasms, pediatric kidney tumors (neuroblastoma and Wilm's tumor/nephroblastoma), lower urinary tract tumors, and prostate tumors are all vital to know for USMLE Step 1.

Benign Kidney Neoplasms

Benign renal neoplasms include renal papillary adenoma, angiomyolipoma, and oncocytoma. Renal papillary adenoma is a common benign epithelial tumor, typically small (<1.5 cm) and often discovered incidentally during autopsy. In histology, it consists of branching papillary or gland-like structures without significant cytologic atypia. Angiomyolipoma, the most common benign renal tumor, arises from perivascular epithelioid cells and is composed of three tissue components: blood vessels, smooth muscle, and mature adipose tissue. It may occur in association with tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC), a disorder caused by mutations in the tumor suppressor genes TSC1 or TSC2 and characterized by lesions in multiple organs such as the brain, heart, and skin. Although many angiomyolipomas are asymptomatic, larger tumors may cause hematuria or retroperitoneal hemorrhage due to fragile abnormal vessels. In histology, they show myoid spindle cells, epithelioid cells, adipose tissue, and thick-walled sclerotic blood vessels. Oncocytoma accounts for approximately 10% of renal neoplasms and is a benign epithelial tumor arising from intercalated (alpha) cells of the collecting ducts. Clinically, it may present with hematuria, flank pain, or an abdominal mass, and it is often difficult to distinguish from renal cell carcinoma. Oncocytoma tumors can grow up to about 12 cm in diameter. Microscopically, oncocytomas display a nested (alveolar) architecture composed of large cells with abundant eosinophilic, granular cytoplasm rich in mitochondria and uniform round nuclei with prominent nucleoli.

Renal Cell Carcinoma

Renal cell carcinoma (RCC) arises from the renal tubular epithelium and is the most common renal malignancy in adults. The most frequent subtype is clear cell renal cell carcinoma, accounting for approximately 70% of cases, and it is strongly associated with loss or mutation of the tumor suppressor gene VHL (von Hippel-Lindau) on chromosome 3, leading to tumor development. The second most common subtype is papillary RCC (about 10-15%), characterized histologically by papillary growth of cuboidal or low columnar cells and commonly associated with genetic alterations such as trisomy 7 and 17 and loss of the Y chromosome; activation of the MET proto-oncogene is particularly important in hereditary forms of papillary RCC. Many hereditary renal cancers involve identifiable genetic mutations, primarily VHL mutations (most high-yield for RCC on Step). In addition to tumors originating from renal parenchyma, primary tumors of the renal pelvis may occur and typically resemble urothelial (transitional cell) carcinomas of the bladder. Clinically, renal malignancies present with hematuria, flank pain, or as an abdominal mass on imaging, and prognosis largely depends on tumor stage and histologic subtype.

RCC can cause widespread metastasis before the onset of symptoms, which is why it is called a silent malignancy. Metastasis is primarily to the lungs, with bones being the second most likely location. It can cause many paraneoplastic syndromes such as polycythemia (increased EPO), hypercalcemia, Cushing syndrome, hypertension, abnormal hormone production, feminization/masculinization due to effects on androgen receptors, and amyloidosis.

RCC is typical in male patients 50-60. It can also lead to renal vein invasion and perinephric fat invasion. Risk factors of RCC include tobacco use, obesity, hypertension, estrogen therapy, asbestos, heavy metals, petroleum, CKD/ESRD, acquired cystic disease, and tuberous sclerosis. Hereditary RCC is purely genetic, and is much rarer than the sporadic form. It can be due to defects in VHL, the FH gene, MET protooncogene mutations, and BHD.

Clear cell carcinoma is the most common type of RCC (75% of renal cell cancers). Papillary is about 15%, and chromophobe carcinoma is about 5%. Xp11 translocation carcinoma, also known as collecting duct carcinoma, is far rarer.

Clear cell carcinoma is a neoplastic malignancy that originates in the PCT. On gross morphology, it grows as a spherical yellow, fatty mass with areas of gray/white necrosis. It is often seen near the cortex and poles of the kidney. The tumor itself can also invade the renal vein and grow in the IVC up to the right atria. In histology, the cells of CCC are well differentiated and have a polygonal shape with abundant, clear cytoplasm. While rare, poorly differentiated CCC is also possible. CCC is due to a loss of the VHL gene on chromosome 3p; this leads to HIF-1 overexpression and uncontrolled cell growth.

Papillary carcinoma is a type of RCC that originates in the DCT. It is usually cystic and hemorrhagic. In histology, it appears with scant stroma (low amounts of ECM, a lot of cells) with low columnar/cuboidal cells in papillary formation (look like fingers) and foamy macrophages. PC has both sporadic and hereditary forms, with MET gain of function being the primary cause of both. Hereditary PC is caused by Trisomy 7, and sporadic PC is caused by trisomy 7,17, or a loss of the Y chromosome.

Chromophobe carcinoma originates from the intercalated cells of collecting ducts; this makes it very difficult to distinguish from oncocytoma. In histology, it presents as sheets of pale cells with very dark nuclei and a perinuclear halo (bright surrounding of the nuclei). Chromosomal losses and hypodiploidy predispose to this malignancy.

Xp11 translocation RCC is due to the translocation of Xp11, overexpressing the TFE3 gene that is a transcription factor, causing uncontrolled cell proliferation. Occurs commonly in young adults. In histology, papillary structures with clear cells can be seen. On IHC, this cancer stains positive for TFE3.

Renal Collecting Duct Carcinoma

A rare form of renal malignancy, RCDC originates in the collecting duct and causes a great, yellow mass in the medullary region of the kidney. It is associated with many different chromosome losses and deletions.

Urothelial Carcinoma of the Renal Pelvis

While most urothelial cancers occur in the bladder, it is possible for UC to form in the renal pelvis. This accounts for 5-10% of all renal tumors. Obstruction of the urinary pathway by this tumor causes unilateral hydronephrosis. Appears as a nodular mass on gross morphology.

Pediatric Kidney Tumors

These renal tumors arise from immature renal cells and are present in infants and young children. Wilm's tumor is most common, presenting in patients 2-5 years old. Neuroblastoma is another immature tumor and is the most common tumor in infants under 1 year old.

Neuroblastoma

Neuroblastoma originates from neural crest cells of the sympathetic ganglia and the adrenal medulla. It is associated with a gain-of-function ALK gene that causes uncontrolled proliferation. Common features include abdominal mass, fever, and weight loss. Metastasis is common to the liver, lungs, bone, and bone marrow. Blueberry muffin rash is also common. Bone pain, respiratory, and GI symptoms are also seen in older children.

Labs show elevated blood catecholamines and elevated metabolites of these catecholamines (VMA, HVA, 3MT). It is a similar malignancy to pheochromocytoma, but also presents without hypertension. A bone marrow biopsy shows small blue cells with high N:C ratios and rosettes (clusters of cells surrounding a fibrotic core).

Children under 18 months typically have good prognosis regardless of stage. Once neuroblastoma crosses the midline (stage 3) and reaches contralateral lymph nodes, prognosis becomes poor. If amplification of the MYCN protooncogene is found, prognosis becomes extremely poor regardless of stage.

Neuroblastoma in the sympathetic ganglia presents as clusters of eosinophilic cells with prominent nuclei. Slight differentiation into neurons can also be seen in histology. Adrenal Neuroblastoma creates fine neuropil (ECM) and sheets of dark, pleomorphic small cells that form Homer-wright pseudorosettes. These tumors stain positive for synaptophysin and chromogranin.

Nephroblastoma

Nephroblastoma, aka Wilms tumor, is distinct from neuroblastoma. It originates from metanephric mesenchyme, and is typically unilateral with a good prognosis (treated with radiation). Features of Wilms include large abdominal mass, hypertension, GI obstruction, hematuria, nausea, and vomiting. Pulmonary metastasis is also common in presentation. Labs show normal catecholamines, differentiating it from neuroblastoma.

Over 90% of cases of Wilms tumor are sporadic without associated congenital syndromes. Wilms tumors are caused by defects in the tumor suppressor genes WT1 and WT2 on chromosome 11. A mutation/deletion of WT1 can cause WAGR syndrome and Deny-Drash syndrome. A WT2 deletion or loss of function causes Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (organomegaly, asymmetric body growth, omphalocele, macroglossia, adrenal cytomegaly).

A Wilms tumor forms a very neat sphere with necrosis, cysts, and bleeding all secondary to the growth. In histology, the tumor has a triphasic cellular pattern with blastemal cells (tightly packed and dark-stained), stroma, and epithelial cells. When TP53 mutations are present (worse prognosis), abnormal mitosis can be observed with hyperchromic, pleomorphic nuclei.

Tumors of the Lower Urinary Tract

A ureter fibroepithelial polyp is a very uncommon benign tumor that forms in mesenchymal CT and only occurs in children. Urothelial carcinoma is far more common and high yield.

Urothelial carcinoma (transitional cell carcinoma) is the most common tumor subtype in the lower urinary tract. It most commonly involves the bladder and is most common in males 50-70. Cigarette smoke is a major risk factor; other risk factors include aryl amines (2-naphthylamine, found in industrial exposure), schistosoma haematobium, and cyclophosphamide with radiation therapy. Causes painless hematuria and can progress into an invasive tumor if TP53 or RB inactivations are present. There are many variants of urothelial tumors.

Urothelial papillomas are largely benign. Papillary urothelial neoplasms of low malignant potential (PUNLMP) are also low risk. Low and high grade urothelial carcinomas can often progress to metastatic cancer, and carcinoma in situ (CIS, also called flat urothelial carcinoma) can progress to metastatic cancer as well.

Urothelial papillomas are rare and occur in younger patients. In histology, they appear with abundant transitional cells and no cell differentiation/signs of atypia. PUNLMP appears thicker than a regular papilloma, and has higher cellularity.

Precursor lesions to urothelial carcinoma are urothelial papillomas that form due to RAS gain or FGFR3 loss mutations. Flat urothelial carcinoma (CIS) comes from squamous cell metaplasia and can progress to invasive carcinoma due to TP53 and RB mutations.

Low grade papillary urothelial carcinoma has fibrovascular cores and is lined with urothelium. It is noninvasive and localized to the outer, luminal bladder. Cells maintain their shape and have prominent nuclei with normal umbrella cell localization. High grade papillary urothelial carcinoma shows pleomorphic cells on histology with necrosis and scattered umbrella cells. High grade papillary urothelial carcinoma shows clumps of pleomorphic cells on pap smears. These cells have high N:C ratios.

Urothelial carcinoma in situ is a flat noninvasive urothelial carcinoma that displays pleomorphism, cellular atypia, and loss of tissue polarity. It is also called fat urothelial carcinoma, as it appears as a yellow fibrotic mass in the urinary tract. Most untreated CIS progress to invasive cancer. Has a loss of function of TP53 or RB. Muscle invasion leads to poor prognosis for this type of cancer (as well as all carcinomas).

Urothelial tumors can be treated with excision; high grade tumors can be treated with the pharmaceutical bacteria Mycobacterium bovis BCG. There is a high risk of recurrence and progression with all urothelial tumors.

The urethra can also form tumors; a Urethral caruncle is a non-neoplastic ulceration that forms due to inflamed granulation tissue. Urothelial papillomas are also possible, as well as carcinoma of the urethra (which is very uncommon).

Prostate Tumors

Benign prostate hyperplasia (BPH) is driven by DHT-inducing growth; stromal cells make DHT from testosterone using 5alpha reductase, causing an increase in prostate growth factor. BPH causes obstruction of the urethra with subsequent hydronephrosis. 5alpha-reductase inhibitors can be used to decrease the amount of circulating DHT. TURP (transurethral resection of the prostate) can be used in severe cases. BPH forms in the transitional zone of the prostate.

Prostate cancer forms in the periphery of the prostate. It is the most common malignancy in older males and is a silent cancer. It has a grade-dependent prognosis and causes testosterone resistance and decreased production. It is caused by unhealthy dietary exposures, chronic inflammation, androgens, and MYC gain of function. These cause p27 and PTEN loss leading to metastatic prostatic adenocarcinoma. Androgen receptor mutations, TP53 and RB mutations, and MYC amplification make this cancer more aggressive. This cancer appears gray-white with poor borders on gross morphology. On histology, the prostate has glandular architecture with atypical cells and nuclei, enlarged nuclei, dark cytoplasm, and the persistence of benign glands surrounding the neoplastic cells.

Pharmacology

Diuretics are a class of medication that causes natriuresis (urination). Typically, diuretics manipulate some aspect of the renal tubule/nephron. They are used for treating edema and hypertension.

Carbonic Anhydrase inhibitor (acetazolamide)

Acetazolimide is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor used for treating glaucoma (it decreases the production of aqueous humor) and as a prophylaxis for altitude sickness. Its adverse effects are metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, paresthesia (pins and needles), and decreased excretion of ammonia. Inhibiting carbonic anhydrase leads to increased urinary excretion of water, sodium, potassium, and bicarbonate.

Loop Diuretics (bumetanide, furosemide, torsemide, ethacrynic acid)

These are very strong diuretics that work at the NKCC channel in the thick ascending loop of Henle. They decrease the reabsorption of Na+, K+, and Cl-. Can be used to treat edema, hyperkalemia, and hypercalcemia. Adverse effects include ototoxicity, hyperuricemia, hypotension, hypokalemia, and hypomagnesemia.

Loop diuretics maintain their function even in patients with poor renal function. They do not cause a decrease in peripheral resistance of blood vessels like thiazide diuretics do; they lower blood pressure by rapidly decreasing blood volume. They are the most commonly used diuretic in heart failure. While they do not improve the survival of patients with heart failure, they do relieve symptoms.

Thiazides (Chlorothiazide, hydrochlorothiazide)

These diuretics inhibit the Na+/Cl- cotransporter in the early DCT causing increased excretion of sodium, potassium, and magnesium. Increased calcium reabsorption occurs. Also causes the relaxation of arteriolar smooth muscle. Thiazides are used for hypertension, congestive heart failure, and idiopathic hypercalciuria. Adverse effects include "hyperGLUC" which are: hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, hyperuricemia, and hypercalcemia. Thiazide-like diuretics (chlorthalidone, metolazone, indapamide) work the same way as regular thiazide diuretics, they just have a different structure.

K+ Sparing Diuretics (Spironolactone, Eplerenone, Amiloride, Triameterene (SEAT))

Spironolactone and eplerenone are diuretics that antagonize aldosterone at intracellular cytoplasmic mineralocorticoid receptors. This decreased the basolateral Na+/K+ antiporter, causing increased sodium secretion and potassium retention. These drugs are used to treat hyperaldosteronism, heart failure, hypertension, and polycystic ovary syndrome. Adverse effects include gynecomastia, menstrual irregularities, and hyperkalemia.

Triamterene and amiloride are ENaC antagonists at the DCT and collecting duct. They are used as antihypertensives (usually in combination with a thiazide diuretic) and can cause hyperkalemia.

These diuretics are used with loop and thiazide diuretics to offset K+ loss. Spironolactone is also used to decrease cardiac remodeling in heart failure.

Osmotic Diuretics

Mannitol is a substance that is filtered by the kidneys and not reabsorbed. It serves to increase the osmolarity of the tubular fluid, retaining water and increasing diuresis. It is used to maintain urine flow and decrease intracranial pressure.

ACE Inhibitors (-pril)

These drugs include lisinopril, enalapril, benazepril, and fosinopril. They inhibit the formation of ATI to ATII. This causes a decrease in peripheral vascular resistance, an increase in bradykinin, an increase in NO and prostacyclin that leads to vasodilation, and a decreased secretion of aldosterone, inhibiting RAAS and decreasing sodium and water retention. This class of medication is used to treat hypertension, diabetic nephropathy, albuminuria in CKD, decrease mortality post-MI, and decrease mortality in patients with heart failure. Note: ACEIs are teratogenic and therefore contraindicated in pregnant patients.

Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs, they end in -sartan)

These drugs include losartan, irbesartan, and valsartan. They compete for the AT1 receptor that Angiotensin II binds to. They do not impact bradykinin, and therefore have a lower incidence of cough.

Renin Inhibitor

Aliskiren is a direct inhibitor of renin. It causes vasodilation and blocks the RAAS system. Used to treat hypertension and causes hyperkalemia, cough, and angioedema.

Alpha 1 Adrenergic Blockers (-osin)

These drugs include terazosin, doxazosin, tamsulosin, alfuzosin, and silodosin. Since -osins block the alpha receptor, they are responsible for vasodilation and relaxation of sympathetic stimuli. They are used to treat hypertension and BPH. Adverse effects include orthostatic hypertension, tachycardia, vertigo, headache, and fatigue.

5-alpha Reductase Inhibitors (finasteride and dutasteride)

Inhibition of 5-alpha reductase causes decreased production of DHT from testosterone, shrinking the prostate and improving urine flow. Dutasteride is more potent than finasteride. These drugs can also be used to treat alopecia and male baldness. Adverse effects include sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia, oligospermia, and teratogenic effects.

Erectile Dysfunction

PDE5 inhibitors like avanafil, sildenafil, tadalafil, and vardenafil (-afil) are first-line therapies for ED. Sildenafil and tadalafil are also used to treat pulmonary hypertension. Since erections are maintained by parasympathetic stimulation to the vasculature of the penis, relaxation of the blood vessels causes erection; PDE5 breaks down cGMP, so inhibition causes cGMP to increase, exacerbating relaxation. Adverse effects include headache, flushing, dyspepsia (indigestion), nasal congestion, trouble with color vision, hearing loss, myalgias characterized by back pain, and priapism (long erection).

Alprostadil is a synthetic prostaglandin E1 analog that increases intracellular cAMP in the cavernosal tissue to increase blood flow to the penis. The extent of smooth muscle relaxation actually compresses the veins of the penis, entrapping blood to maintain erection. This drug is actually administered as a shot directly to the penis!

Chronic Kidney Disease

CKD is characterized by decreasing GFR with eventual ESRD. Mostly caused by Diabetes Mellitus, it can also be caused by HTN, glomerulonephritis (IgA, FSGS, membranous glomerulonephropathy, Alport Syndrome), and polycystic kidney disease. CKD presents with uremia that can be alleviated with reduced protein intake and fluid overload that can be treated with fluid restriction and diuretics.

CKD also presents with hyperkalemia which can cause arrhythmia (treated with calcium supplementation). Renal osteodystrophy is commonly seen in patients with CKD, the primary of which being caused by decreased mineralization (known as osteomalacia, which is the softening of the bone due to ineffective vitamin D (the activator of vitamin D, 1alpha hydroxylase, is made in the PCT of the kidney and impaired in CKD)).

Hypocalcemia is common and is treated with calcium and Vitamin D supplementation. Hypocalcemia due to CKD causes secondary hyperparathyroidism (common finding: low Ca++ and high phosphate) which can be treated with parathyroid removal (parathyroidectomy) and Cinacalcet, a drug that antagonizes Ca++ sensors. Tertiary hyperparathyroidism develops due to prolonged secondary hyperparathyroidism, and is characterized by high Ca++ and high phosphate.

CKD also presents with hyperphosphatemia and secondary pruritus (itchy skin) due to the buildup of phosphate salts. Hyperphosphatemia and hypercalcemia cause calcium phosphate crystals to build up and deposit in blood vessels. Calcification of blood vessels further causes necrosis, aka calciphylaxis. Sevelamir and Renagel are phosphate binders used to treat hyperphosphatemia.

CKD is a cause of high anion gap metabolic acidosis. It also causes high blood pressure and requires antihypertensives (ACEi) to alleviate symptoms of HTN, glomerular pressure, and proteinuria. Anemia is also present in CKD (due to lack of EPO synthesis) and is treated with the EPO analog Epoetin Alpha (as well as with iron supplements).

CKD can lead to pericarditis, the accumulation of fluid in the pericardium due to inflammation and uremia. Presents as chest pain, pericardial friction rub, and diffuse ST elevations. Responds well to dialysis. CKD presents with paradoxical increased risk of bleeding and hypercoagulability, with the increased risk of bleeding being due to platelet dysfunction (alleviated by vasopressin, an ADH analog, that releases vWF from endothelial cells) and hypercoagulability due to the loss of antithrombin III in damaged glomeruli.

If CKD progresses to ESRD, a patient must go on dialysis to filter blood. They may also need a kidney transplant. An allograft is a transplant that comes from someone who is not genetically identical (could be a parent, sibling, or stranger). An isograft comes exclusively from an identical twin. An orthotopic transplant is placed where the organ should be. A heterotopic transplant is placed in a different location in the body. Most kidney transplants are heterotropic.

Hyperacute rejection occurs when the recipient has antibodies already formed against the donor allograft (usually the ABO antigen). This is prevented by crossmatching antibodies in the donor and in the recipient (this is why you need to find a "match" before giving a kidney).

Acute rejection occurs via cellular mechanism; the CD4+ cells in the body recognize foreign antigen via the MHC II receptor (and the antigen itself is presented by APCs) and trigger an immune response via IL-2. CD8+ cells are activated and attack the allograft tissue. Acute allograft rejection occurs after the patient is initially sent home after successful transplant; weeks to months later, the patient presents with high creatinine, no findings on imaging, and lymphoid infiltrate on biopsy of the allograft.
